FAQs (Frequently Asked Questions)

  1. What is a clinical trial? A research study to evaluate medical, surgical, or behavioral interventions, determining their safety and effectiveness.
  2. Who can participate in a clinical trial? Participation depends on meeting eligibility criteria like age, gender, medical history, and health status.
  3. What is informed consent? A process where participants learn about the trial's purpose, risks, benefits, and procedures to make an informed decision.
  4. Will I be paid for participating in a clinical trial? Compensation varies by study and may include reimbursement for time, travel, or expenses.
  5. Can I leave a clinical trial after enrolling? Yes, participation is voluntary, and you can withdraw anytime without penalty.
  6. What happens if I experience side effects during the trial? Report them immediately to the research team for guidance and support.
  7. How are participants assigned to groups? Often, participants are assigned randomly to different groups to reduce bias.
  8. Will I know which treatment I am receiving? Some studies are blinded, so participants and researchers may not know who receives which treatment to prevent bias.
  9. How long do clinical trials last? Duration varies, ranging from weeks to years depending on the trial phase and objectives.
  10. What happens after the trial ends? Data is analyzed, and participants may be informed of findings. Continued access to the treatment may be offered in some cases.
  11. Are clinical trials covered by insurance? Insurance coverage varies. Some costs may be covered by the sponsor, while others may be billed to your insurance or out-of-pocket.
  12. What is a placebo, and will I receive one? A placebo is an inactive substance. Not all trials use placebos; this is explained during informed consent.

Glossary of Medical Terms

  • Adverse Event (AE): Any undesirable experience associated with the use of a medical product in a patient.
  • Clinical Trial: A research study to evaluate interventions according to a protocol and their effects on health outcomes.
  • Control Group: Participants who do not receive the experimental treatment but may receive a placebo or standard treatment.
  • Eligibility Criteria: Medical or other standards determining a personâ€™s participation in a clinical trial.
  • Informed Consent: The process of learning about a trial and agreeing to participate after understanding the risks and benefits.
  • Institutional Review Board (IRB): A group monitoring human research for ethical conduct.
  • Placebo: An inactive substance used as a control in testing new treatments.
  • Principal Investigator (PI): The person responsible for conducting and overseeing a clinical trial.
  • Protocol: A detailed plan describing the objectives, design, and methods of a clinical trial.
  • Randomization: Assigning participants randomly to different groups to reduce bias.
  • Sponsor: An individual or organization initiating and managing a clinical trial.
  • Study Arm: A subgroup of participants in a clinical trial receiving a specific intervention or no intervention.
  • Trial Phase: A stage of testing a drug or treatment (e.g., Phase I, II, III, IV).
  • Withdrawal: The act of leaving a clinical trial before it is completed.
